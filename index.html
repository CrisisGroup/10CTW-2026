Ten Conflicts to Watch

by Comfort Ero and Richard Atwood

Published 1 January, 2026

The world order as we know it is gone starting 2026. The Western alliances have fallen apart, tariffs have triggered trade wars and the world fragments into competing spheres of influence rather than rules-based cooperation.

SUDAN

The Eritrea-Sudan-Ethiopia triangle is defined by shifting alliances and territorial disputes rooted in the region's complex history. Eritrea and Ethiopia fought a brutal border war that killed tens of thousands, though they signed a peace deal in 2018. Ethiopia's civil war in Tigray saw Eritrean forces intervene alongside Ethiopian federal troops, allegedly committing atrocities. Sudan has faced its own instability, with border tensions with Ethiopia over al-Fasher territory and concerns about the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam.

Gaza

The ceasefire is extremely fragile with frequent violations, and Israel's military chief has already declared the yellow line a "new border" while Israeli forces dig in with fortifications. The year ahead will likely see continued Israeli control over much of Gaza, with little prospect of the planned withdrawals or international stabilization force materializing.

Ukraine

Ukraine negotiations remain deadlocked over territory and NATO membership, with 2026 likely bringing either a frozen conflict requiring permanent European military presence or a controversial deal that rewards Russian aggression. Either outcome forces Europe to dramatically increase defense spending, manage long-term instability on its eastern border, and confront whether collective security guarantees still hold meaning in the post-American order.

IRAN HOUTHIS vs ISRAEL

The Assad regime collapsed in December 2024 after a rapid rebel offensive led by Hayat Tahrir al-Sham captured Damascus, ending over 50 years of Assad family rule. Syria now faces a fragile transition under interim president Ahmed al-Sharaa, with ongoing sectarian violence targeting Alawite and Druze communities, Kurdish-SDF clashes with Turkish-backed forces in the northeast, and the Islamic State exploiting the security vacuum. Israel has conducted extensive airstrikes destroying Syrian military infrastructure and expanded its occupation into the Golan Heights buffer zone, citing security concerns about the new government.
