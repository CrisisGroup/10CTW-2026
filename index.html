Ten Conflicts to Watch in 2026

The world was careening into a dangerous new era well before Donald Trump’s return to the White House. But the risks are getting higher rather than lower in his second term thus far.

by Comfort Ero and Richard Atwood

Published 31 December 2025

President Donald Trump came back to office pledging to bring peace to a world ablaze. He has put himself centre stage in many of the world’s trouble spots. He has brought new energy to peacemaking, after years in which diplomatic efforts to end wars had been flailing.

But Trump has not calmed the global turmoil he decried on the campaign trail – if anything, he has made it worse. His deals have brought respite to some battlefields but no genuine peace anywhere. Meanwhile, his lawlessness and spheres-of-influence politics have given licence to other states tempted by adventurism.

The year ahead thus seems likely to be as bloody as the one drawing to a close. Here are the International Crisis Group’s Ten Conflicts to Watch in 2026.

VENEZUELA

The U.S. has commenced its biggest military build-up in the Caribbean Sea in decades, apparently as part of designs to remove Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro from power. In December, the coast guard began seizing tankers exporting Venezuelan crude in what Trump called a blockade.

If Maduro, who has weathered economic pressure for years, does not bend, an attempt at ousting him by force may be in the offing. But military action is more likely to go wrong than not. It could sow chaos, triggering refugee flight and protracted conflict.

SUDAN

The war in Sudan grinds on toward de facto partition, with the army and paramilitary Rapid Support Forces each controlling swathes of territory. Outside powers are still giving peacemaking too little attention amid the mounting human toll.

Washington should lean on Abu Dhabi to stop arming the RSF and on Riyadh and others to limit supplies to the army. A permanent ceasefire could follow, along with steps to establish a civilian-led transitional government. It will be a tall order to piece Sudan back together, but a truce must come first.

Ethiopia-Eritrea

Meanwhile, two of Sudan’s neighbours, Ethiopia and Eritrea, are trading barbs. Addis Ababa accuses Eritrea of meddling in Ethiopia’s internal conflicts; Asmara suspects landlocked Ethiopia of coveting an Eritrean seaport.

A distracted world is largely ignoring these growing tensions. Considering the stakes, African and other powers should intercede to head off hostilities. The last Ethiopia-Eritrea war killed tens of thousands. Another could be just as deadly. It could also tip the Horn of Africa into all-out conflagration.

Mali and Burkina Faso

In September, militants linked to al-Qaeda imposed a partial blockade on the Malian capital Bamako, highlighting the deepening crisis in the central Sahel. In both Mali and neighbouring Burkina Faso, military regimes have failed to beat back jihadist groups.

Absent drastic change, things look bleak. Authorities in both landlocked countries should consider mending ties with coastal West African states to safeguard routes to seaports. They need to contemplate dialogue with insurgents, too. It’s an uncertain course, but few other options remain.

Ukraine

Russian forces continue to grind ahead, though at tremendous cost and without breaking Ukrainian lines. Ukraine shows no sign of giving up, despite its own significant losses. What’s changed over the past year is less the battlefield than the politics – thanks to Trump’s upending of U.S. support for Ukraine and its president.

How events will unfold is hard to know. The big question is what will happen if Trump, losing patience, tries to impose a deal skewed toward Moscow on Kyiv and its European backers.

SYRIA

Since seizing power in late 2024, Ahmed al-Sharaa has won over a sceptical world. But the new Syrian president’s gains abroad could be undone if he cannot curb sectarian bloodletting at home.

Other challenges are also formidable. Even with sanctions lifted, jump-starting the war-ravaged economy will be difficult. The public is still hopeful but increasingly frustrated with al-Sharaa’s concentration of power. The north east remains outside central control, and the spectre of further Turkish or Israeli intervention is ever present.

Israel-Palestine

The bloodiest war in a century of Arab-Israeli conflict ground to a tenuous halt in October. But Gaza remains in limbo, its surviving population crowded into less than half the strip, much of it reduced to rubble under continued Israeli fire. In the West Bank, the hardest-right government in Israeli history seems bent on annexation.

In Gaza, the question is whether Palestinians can hold together as a society. In the West Bank, it is how fast the territory shrinks, displacement accelerates and the Palestinian Authority’s writ dissolves.

Israel and the U.S. versus Iran and the Houthis

The lull following June’s 12-day war gave all sides a measure of satisfaction: Israel and the U.S. did great damage to the Iranian nuclear and missile programs, while Iran was pleased to remain standing after absorbing the blows. But the core disputes between the sides are unresolved.

Should hostilities resume, both Israel and Iran (with help from the Houthis in Yemen) could launch bigger, deadlier barrages. Instead, the U.S. and Iran should shore up the shaky truce, keeping an eye out for opportunities for a lasting deal.

MYANMAR

The military regime that seized power in 2021, driving Myanmar into civil war, has gained the upper hand after a period in which rebels looked ascendant. China has thrown its weight behind the junta, while other Asian powers are betting that military control will persist after a sham vote installs a nominally civilian government.

The war is far from over, and the regime is widely loathed, but its opponents have no evident strategy for dislodging it. All these developments bode ill for the Rohingya, whether those in Bangladesh or those remaining in Myanmar.

AFGHANISTAN-PAKISTAN

Afghanistan and Pakistan came to blows in early October, after Islamabad attributed a surge in attacks on Pakistani territory to insurgents based across the Afghan border. A ceasefire is holding for now, but bilateral tensions have not eased.

Diplomatic ties are suspended. Trade has ceased. Pakistan is deporting masses of Afghan nationals. The Afghan Taliban, meanwhile, are deepening Pakistani suspicions by forging closer ties with India. A resumption of hostilities is likely should militant violence persist.
