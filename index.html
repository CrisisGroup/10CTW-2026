Ten Conflicts to Watch

by Comfort Ero and Richard Atwood

Published 1 January, 2026

The world order as we know it is gone starting 2026. The Western alliances have fallen apart, tariffs have triggered trade wars and the world fragments into competing spheres of influence rather than rules-based cooperation.

SUDAN

Sudan is facing a complex and rapidly evolving conflict marked by intense fighting between rival military forces and widespread civilian harm. The breakdown of political transition efforts has triggered displacement on a massive scale, disrupted basic services, and deepened existing humanitarian crises across the country. Regional spillover risks and restricted access for aid organizations continue to complicate international response efforts.

Eritrea-Ethiopia

The Eritrea–Ethiopia conflict reflects the fragile aftermath of years of war in northern Ethiopia, where periods of open fighting have given way to uneasy ceasefires. Deep grievances, unresolved political questions, and allegations of widespread abuses have left communities vulnerable and trust in peace processes limited. The long-term stability of the region remains uncertain despite reduced large-scale combat.

Gaza

Gaza remains at the center of a protracted and highly volatile conflict characterized by recurring cycles of violence, civilian casualties, and severe humanitarian strain. Blockades, airstrikes, and armed confrontations have devastated infrastructure and constrained daily life for millions of residents. Political deadlock and regional tensions continue to impede durable solutions.

Ukraine

The war in Ukraine has reshaped European security and triggered one of the largest displacement crises in recent history. Sustained military operations, including attacks on cities and infrastructure, have caused extensive civilian suffering and economic disruption. The conflict has drawn in major international actors and remains a central flashpoint in global geopolitics.

Mali

Mali is grappling with a multifaceted security crisis driven by insurgency, political instability, and weakened state authority. Armed groups operate across large areas, fueling violence against civilians and undermining development efforts. Repeated military takeovers and shifting foreign partnerships have further complicated prospects for lasting stability.

IRAN HOUTHIS vs ISRAEL

Yemen’s conflict has produced one of the world’s most severe humanitarian emergencies, with millions facing food insecurity, displacement, and limited access to healthcare. Although periods of reduced fighting have occurred, political fragmentation and unresolved grievances persist. The country’s recovery remains contingent on sustained diplomatic engagement and improved humanitarian access.

AFGHANISTAN-PAKISTAN

The Afghanistan–Pakistan border region is marked by ongoing insecurity tied to militant activity, cross-border tensions, and shifting political dynamics. Violence and displacement continue to affect local populations, while regional actors struggle to manage security and economic pressures. The situation remains fluid, with limited mechanisms for long-term conflict resolution.

MYANMAR

Myanmar is experiencing widespread internal conflict following the collapse of civilian rule and the expansion of armed resistance across the country. Fighting between the military and multiple opposition groups has displaced large numbers of people and strained humanitarian operations. The crisis shows few signs of resolution as violence becomes increasingly decentralized.

SYRIA

Syria’s conflict has entered a prolonged phase of fragmentation, with multiple actors controlling different parts of the country. While frontlines have largely stabilized, economic collapse, displacement, and humanitarian needs remain acute. Political negotiations have stalled, leaving millions in a state of prolonged uncertainty.

VENEZUELA AND THE U.S.

Venezuela’s crisis stems from prolonged political confrontation and economic decline rather than active warfare, yet its human impact has been profound. Shortages of basic goods, governance disputes, and social unrest have driven large-scale migration across the region. Efforts at political dialogue continue intermittently amid deep mistrust.
